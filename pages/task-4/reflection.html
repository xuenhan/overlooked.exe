What You Did:

In this task, you navigated an online shopping interface under two different conditions. First, you were asked to locate the Air Fryer, add it to your cart, and complete the checkout process. You then repeated the same task after cognitive barriers were introduced, where familiar labels and terminology were replaced with more complex and unfamiliar language, increasing the difficulty of navigation and decision-making.

What Changed?

The first time, the interface used clear, familiar language you could act on quickly. In the barrier version, button labels, section headings, navigation terms, and confirmation messages were all replaced with abstract, bureaucratic language. The layout, your account details, and the task itself remained identical; only the words changed.

Baseline Experience Barrier Experience
Clear button labels like "Add to Cart" and "Proceed to Checkout" Abstract labels like "Queue Allocation", "Immediate Dispatch", and "Initiate Fulfilment"
Clear and intuitive navigation with recognizable layout and actions Increased cognitive load due to information overload, jargon, and unfamiliar terminology
Confident next steps at every stage Competing actions of equal visual weight with no clear primary option
Straightforward checkout process with clear next steps Ambiguous workflow requiring users to interpret meaning before proceeding

Who Might Be Affected?

  • Users with cognitive disabilities who rely on consistent and predictable language to navigate interfaces
  • Users with attention or processing differences who cannot easily decode unfamiliar terminology under time pressure
  • Users experiencing anxiety disorders for whom unclear confirmation states and competing options create genuine distress
  • Older adults who may not have the prior experience needed to interpret abstract or technical interface language
  • Anyone navigating in a non-native language where bureaucratic phrasing is significantly harder to parse

Consider Your Own Experience:

  • Did you hesitate before clicking a button because you weren't sure what it would do?
  • Did the familiar navigation labels make it harder to determine where to find the correct item?
  • Did you feel confident the order actually went through at the end?
  • Did the language make a familiar task feel unexpectedly difficult?
💡

Why This Matters

💡

Shopping, checking out, and confirming a purchase are core interactions in almost every digital commerce platform.

When labels are vague or inconsistent, users cannot rely on recognition to guide their decisions; they must actively decode every step. For users with cognitive differences, this is not a minor inconvenience. It is a barrier that can make completing even simple tasks unreliable or impossible.

Accessibility isn't just about structure and layout. It is about ensuring that language itself is inclusive, so that every user can act with confidence, not confusion.
